# In Accord
### A manifesto for agency work in the LLM era

A client you did good work for says: *"We've decided to go in a different direction."*

They can't say what direction.

Two weeks later they have hired an agency whose pitch ChatGPT could have produced in five minutes. Images that look art-directed. Video that used to take a crew weeks. A framework that sounds like a framework. A strategy document that reads like strategy. None of it will work. All of it won the room.

You drive home running the tape. Maybe the creative. Maybe the price. Maybe you should have used more AI. A voice that has been getting louder all year says: *maybe we just need a better idea.*

---

That voice is what breaks agencies. Not the client. Not the creative. Not the price.

It is the belief that there is *one right thing* — one idea, one tool, one hire — that, once found, fixes everything. Every agency that breaks in the LLM era breaks through this voice.

The pressure outside matches the voice inside. Clients leave after eighteen months despite good work. *"We can get that cheaper."* Thinner agencies win pitches with posture alone. Some clients don't want depth at all — *"I don't care about the brand, I care about the number"* is a real sentence in a real meeting.

We know this loop. We lived in it for years.

Brand matters on both sides of the table. For the agency, it is the most important asset — the thing no tool copies and no subscription ships. For the client, it is the same — the thing that compounds into the number they will care about three years from now. The answer to *"I don't care about brand"* is not to drop brand work. It is to keep building, measure in the language the client trusts, and let the lever show its work.

---

What you want is not what the voice chases.

You don't want a better pitch deck. You want to not need one.

You don't want a better idea. You want to be the agency called first when something breaks.

You don't want to use more AI. You want to use it so your agency gets more *your* agency, not more generic.

The chase for a better idea is the voice talking. It is not you.

---

We made a different choice.

The billable hour stood in for expertise for twenty years. The hour was scarce. The knowledge behind it was hard to copy. AI did not make knowledge cheap. It made *delivery* of knowledge cheap. The hour is still scarce. The deliverable is not.

A seat of ChatGPT in a billable-hour agency is a faster treadmill. So is a seat of Claude. The tools commoditize by the month. If your differentiator is which AI your team uses, you do not have a differentiator. You have a subscription.

AI is only the latest aisle in a supermarket that got enormous while nobody watched. The Chiefmartec landscape counts more than fourteen thousand martech tools. Analytics, attribution, automation, enrichment, measurement, orchestration. More every quarter. Your edge cannot live in which ten you picked. It has to live somewhere those fourteen thousand tools cannot reach.

So we stopped.

We stopped trying to find better creative. We started trying to see what was actually broken above the layer where our work happened. We stopped treating the agency as a labor pool. We started building it as an operating system.

The operating system has six parts. Each has a name.

- **Ladder** — where the agency stands on its AI adoption journey, honestly named.
- **Layers** — where in the client's business the problem actually lives.
- **Trail** — a source you can point to for every claim.
- **Rhythms** — loops around projects, reviews, retros.
- **Memory** — what the system remembers so people don't have to.
- **Accord** — humans and AI sharpening each other on real work.

What we rejected:

- The billable hour as a unit of value
- Tools without protocols
- Delivery without loops
- Frameworks without memory
- Cleverness without evidence
- "AI transformation" as a line item on a proposal

We have not finished rejecting these. We reject them one engagement at a time. Anyone who says the billable hour is dead at their agency is selling you something.

The operating system has six parts. The practitioner who runs it has seven habits. These are the seven.

1. **Diagnose before you deliver.** See the constraint above your work before you propose the work. Every brief starts with a diagnosis, not a deck. Present the finding clearly. Show the evidence. Ask the client where it doesn't match what they see on the ground. Inquiry is how professionals work together. Apologetic tentativeness is weakness wearing humility as a costume.

2. **Show the trail.** Every claim has a source you can point to. When you don't have one, you say *"I don't know yet, but I'll find out."* AI hands you precise-looking numbers — percentages, benchmarks, charts with trend lines — that turn out to be invented. Confident wrong is its default output. The trail is the only defense.

3. **Close every loop.** Projects are real — start dates, end dates, invoices. What cannot end with the project is the learning. Close with a post-mortem: what worked, what broke, what the next project starts from. A project that ends with an invoice extracted value. A project that ends with a loop built it — for both sides.

4. **Work in accord with the machine.** Use AI as a teammate whose output you can inspect. Keep your thinking legible to it. Keep its output legible to you. Neither replaces the other. Automation replaces the human. Accord assumes the human is irreplaceable and makes the machine useful to them — and to the next human who walks into the room cold.

5. **Carry lessons through people.** Agencies do not learn. People do. Patterns travel in conversation, in review, in a practitioner who sees the same shape a year later in a different business. And they travel through each practitioner differently — a pattern named by one lens is provisional until a second lens confirms it. Know whose lens you are reading. Systems help, but they serve the connection between people — they do not replace it.

6. **Count on the people in the room.** Strategy that counts *on* people holds. Strategy that counts *around* them collapses. The client, their partners, their franchisees, the ops manager who will live with what you recommend — they sit in the room, not across the table. They carry context you will never have. Ask their questions. Write their names into the plan. And know who they are — cognitive shape is as real as a role title, and a seat that fits the person holds while one that does not drifts. *Együtt* — together — is a working relationship, not a slogan.

7. **Sharpen the instrument.** The agency is the product. Invest in what compounds — craft, brand, memory, accord. Retire what does not — the billable hour, the tool-of-the-month, the deck nobody remembers. The renewal is the work.

---

Here is what happened.

We started losing clients. The work was good. The clients still left. For a long time we thought it was the creative, the price, the pitch. It was none of those. We were losing them because we could not see — or name — the thing actually broken above our work. Once we understood that, we could start to reverse it.

Pitch meetings became short conversations. We came with a diagnosis, not a deck. Clients said things they had never said out loud, because nobody had given them a framework to say them with.

Clients stopped drowning in documents. The agency stopped shipping another deck. We started maintaining a system — one place where what was decided last month is still findable today. Remembering moved off the owner's desk.

Every agency will soon have access to the same leading AI. Equal access is the new floor. What sets one agency apart sits *above* the tool. It sits where agency work has always lived — in the craft, the client knowledge, the rhythms, the frameworks, the scar tissue.

None of this is new work. It is the work agencies have always done at their best. What started compounding once we wrote it down:

- **Client knowledge a good account lead carries in their head** — that the CMO will not read past page three, that last year's rebrand still stings, that the sales team resents marketing for something said in Q2. No AI starts a Monday knowing this.
- **The discipline of showing your work.** AI invents. A good agency has always insisted on the source.
- **The rhythms that make an agency an agency** — weekly status, monthly reviews, quarterly retros, the Friday post-mortem. No AI installs itself into a calendar.
- **The house frameworks built over years** — the discovery refined across fifty clients, the brief with sections nobody else writes. AI gives generic. The house gives specific.
- **The scar tissue** — the twenty ways a campaign can die. The client type that always bounces in month three. No AI has lived through this. A practitioner has.

An agency that rents AI gets what everyone gets. An agency that runs on one gets what compounds — the craft it already had, plus a way to keep it.

Some tool vendors already understood the agency's calling. A handful of platforms were built on an insight the rest of the category missed: performance numbers are useful only when a practitioner can ask questions of them in context. An agency is orchestrating a customer journey, not maintaining a list. These vendors know their place is *inside* the substrate, not around the agency. We build the operating system on top of what they make.

---

> **We were not losing clients because our work was bad. We were losing them because we could not see — or name — the constraint that lived above our work. The moment we got the tools to see it, we stopped being interchangeable.**

The tool was never the edge. The ability to see above the tool was.

This is what we mean by *accord*: the agency's thinking is legible to the machine, the machine's output is legible to the humans, and both get sharper every time either touches real work.

Accord is not automation. Automation replaces the human. Accord assumes the human is irreplaceable, and makes the machine useful to them — and to the next human who walks into the room cold.

---

If you are losing clients after eighteen months and you don't know why — it is not your creative. Not your pricing. The constraint lives above your work, and nobody has given you the tools to name it.

Until you have those tools, every optimization is a guess. Every pitch is a performance.

The voice from the first page goes quiet when the agency becomes the kind of thing that can see the constraint above its own work, name it to a client, and still be in the room twelve months later because the naming was the reason it stayed.

Most practitioners reading this are on a ladder that runs from *"I sometimes ask ChatGPT to write an email"* to *"my agency runs a live diagnostic engine on real client data."* Ten levels. No shame on any of them. The practitioner at Level 0 is not behind the one at Level 8. They are earlier in the same work.

The credential here is practice that can be inspected. A practitioner who runs their agency as an operating system has artifacts — a registry, a log, a set of loops, a network of findings. We share artifacts. We sign patterns. We review each other's reasoning. We let the work speak. If certification ever makes sense in this movement, it will be earned by what a practitioner has built, not by a seat warmed in a course.

If the problem an agency solves fits inside a single person's head, the person will leave and take the agency with them. If it fits inside a folder of SOPs, the folder rots. If it lives in an operating system — versioned, inspectable, run in accord between humans and machines — the agency survives its founders and the tools they started with.

That is the work. That is the movement.
