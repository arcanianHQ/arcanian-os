# Why Agencies Break in the LLM Era

A longer look at what the manifesto claims in a thousand words.

---

## The agency model had one good trick

For twenty years, the agency business ran on a quiet trade.

You paid for hours. What you actually got, when the agency was good, was expertise — the hard-won judgment of people who had run fifty campaigns, read ten thousand briefs, watched a hundred launches live and die. The hour was the unit because the hour was the thing the agency could meter. It was also the thing the client could understand on an invoice.

But the hour was never really what was being sold. It was a proxy.

The proxy worked because two things were true. First, the hour was scarce — a senior creative director could only be in one meeting at a time. Second, the knowledge behind the hour was hard to copy. You could hire someone from another agency and get a fraction of it. You could read a book about marketing and get a little more. But you could not clone the twenty years of pattern recognition that made a senior's answer land three seconds after the brief arrived.

The billable hour worked because the proxy held.

---

## What the LLM changed

Large language models did not make the underlying knowledge cheap. A senior creative director's twenty years of pattern recognition is still twenty years of pattern recognition. What the LLM made cheap is the **delivery** of knowledge that *looks like* that pattern recognition.

A client in 2020 who wanted a positioning framework paid an agency for three weeks of strategist time. The output was a deck. The deck was the deliverable; the strategist's thinking was what the deck was supposed to encode.

A client in 2026 who wants a positioning framework can open ChatGPT, type a reasonable prompt, and get a deck in five minutes. The deck is the deliverable. It sounds plausible. It reads like strategy. It has frameworks with capitalised names. A junior marketing coordinator on the client side can put it into a slide template and walk into a meeting with it.

Is it actually good? Often no. Sometimes yes. The point is not whether the output is equivalent — the point is that *the client can no longer tell*, at a glance, whether the three-week strategist version is meaningfully better than the five-minute LLM version. And when the client cannot tell the difference, the pricing collapses toward the cheaper option.

The hour is still scarce. The hour's output is not.

---

## The loop this creates

The pressure shows up in five specific ways, and they reinforce each other.

**Clients leave despite good work.** An eighteen-month churn cycle becomes common. The work the agency is proud of does not prevent the client from being courted by someone promising "AI-native strategy" at a third of the price.

**Prices push down.** Not because the competition's work is better — because a credible-looking AI-generated alternative is now the baseline against which the agency's quote is compared.

**Thinner agencies win pitches with posture alone.** The agency that arrives with a framework-that-sounds-like-a-framework, a chart-that-looks-like-it-has-a-trend, a document-that-reads-like-strategy — wins the room against an agency arriving with actual research. Not always. But often enough to hurt.

**Some clients flatly reject depth.** *"I don't care about the brand, I care about the number"* becomes a real sentence in a real meeting. The client is not wrong to be suspicious of brand claims — too many agencies sold brand work for too long without ever showing it moved anything measurable. But the client has also collapsed the investment horizon to a window where nothing that compounds can be paid for.

**The agency starts doubting its own work.** A voice inside the agency that has been getting louder for a year says: *maybe we just need a better idea. Maybe it's our creative. Maybe we should use more AI ourselves.* The voice is the most dangerous thing, because it tells the agency to keep solving the wrong problem.

The loop is real. It is killing agencies that do good work.

---

## Why "use more AI" is the wrong answer

When the pressure hits, the obvious move is to adopt the tool that is commoditising you.

An agency buys fifty ChatGPT seats. Trains the team on prompts. Publishes a LinkedIn post about its AI-native transformation. Opens a pitch deck with *"We use generative AI to accelerate strategic output."*

This does not work. Two reasons.

**First, the tool is not the differentiator.** AI is a seat. Every agency has it or will soon. The client also has it. A credential that says *"we use AI"* is a credential every competitor — and every in-house team — can also claim. When a capability becomes universal, it stops being an edge. It becomes a floor.

**Second, more AI inside a broken structure makes the breakage faster.** If the agency was already shipping decks that looked like strategy without being strategy, adding AI makes the agency ship those decks five times as fast. Faster wrong is not progress.

An agency trying to solve LLM commoditisation by adopting LLMs is running on a treadmill that keeps speeding up. At some point it cannot keep up and the client leaves for someone faster or cheaper — because that was the only axis the agency was competing on.

The answer is not more of the tool. The answer is a different unit of value.

---

## The agency that survives

The agency that survives the LLM era is not the one with the best AI stack. It is the one that moved its unit of value above the tool.

Specifically: the surviving agency sells **the ability to see what the client cannot see**. The constraint above the client's own work. The layer of the business where the real bottleneck lives, which is almost never the layer the client assumed.

This is the thing AI does not do well, and probably will not do well for a long time. Not because AI is not smart enough — it is already smart enough to draft most things. But because naming the constraint above a client's work requires:

- **Context the AI doesn't have.** Not "I read about your business" context. "I watched your account lead take notes in a meeting where your VP of sales and your VP of marketing disagreed about whether Q3 was a pricing problem or a positioning problem" context.
- **Evidence the AI cannot source itself.** Real numbers from the client's real platforms. Real quotes from real meetings. Real patterns across a real portfolio of real clients over real years.
- **A framework for knowing where to look.** The client-business ladder that runs from founder identity and core purpose at the top, through offer and channels and customer fit at the bottom. Naming which layer the constraint sits at, and being able to show why.
- **The courage to say it plainly.** Most client-facing work hedges. The agency that actually survives says the constraint out loud, early in the engagement, and is still in the room twelve months later because the naming was the reason the client kept them.

Notice how much of this is things the agency has always done when it was at its best. Account planners always backed up claims with evidence. Creative directors always demanded strategic rationale for the work. Senior account leads always carried the context the AI cannot see. The discipline of the surviving agency is not new. It is just harder to find under the commoditisation pressure.

---

## What changes next

The shift is from *"we execute campaigns"* to *"we run a system that sees what the client cannot see, and the campaigns fall out of it."*

This is the operating-system move. An agency-as-operating-system has:

- **Continuous habits** — how a practitioner works every day, at every layer. Seven of them, in the practitioner's hands.
- **Rituals at each layer's natural rhythm** — weekly for channels, quarterly for offer and customer fit, annually for founder belief. Scheduled pauses to check the work is still honest at the cadence each layer demands.
- **Artifacts** — the one-page diagnosis, the scorecard, the pattern log, the accountability map. Written outputs the agency, the practitioners, and the client can all inspect.
- **Memory** — what the system remembers so people don't have to. The substrate that compounds across clients and survives the departure of any single senior.
- **Accord** — the agency's thinking legible to the machine; the machine's output legible to the agency. Both sharpen with use.

None of this is new work. It is the work agencies have always done at their best. What is new is that it used to live in senior people's heads, in filing cabinets, and in the muscle memory of a team that happened to still be there. Now it can be written down in a form the next human *and* the next AI can both read.

An agency that rents AI gets what everyone gets. An agency that runs on one — that uses AI as a teammate inside an operating system the agency actually owns — gets something else. It gets the craft it already had, plus a way to keep it.

That is what AOS is for.
